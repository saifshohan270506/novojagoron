**Slide 1 — TITLE / HOOK (Dynamic)**
**THE LIVING SKIN OF EARTH**
The ground we walk on is not a passive shell, but a dynamic, ever-changing membrane. Forged by fire, sculpted by water, and transformed by pressure—this is the story of Earth's rocky skin and its endless cycle of renewal.
*(Visual: A stunning macro photograph of banded gneiss or intricate crystalline granite, showing nature's artistry in stone.)*

**Slide 2 — THE THIN SHELL**
Earth's crust is astonishingly thin. **Oceanic crust** is ~7 km thick (dense, young basalt). **Continental crust** is ~35-70 km thick (buoyant, ancient granite). Together, they form less than **1%** of Earth's volume—the delicate skin on the planetary forge.
*(Visual: A scaled diagram of Earth as a circle, with the crust magnified as a thin, highlighted line. Two insets show the different crust types.)*

**Slide 3 — OCEANIC CRUST: The Dense Floor**
Dark, fine-grained **basalt**. Born from mantle melts at mid-ocean ridges, it is geologically young (never older than ~200 million years) and destined to be recycled back into the mantle at subduction zones. The transient floor of the ocean.
*(Visual: A close-up photo of dark, vesicular basalt, perhaps with pillow shapes from underwater formation.)*

**Slide 4 — CONTINENTAL CRUST: The Ancient Foundation**
Light, coarse-grained **granite**. Forms the enduring cores of continents, some rocks over 4 billion years old. Its buoyancy prevents it from being easily subducted, making continents the permanent archives of Earth's history.
*(Visual: A polished slab of speckled granite, showing large crystals of quartz, feldspar, and mica.)*

**Slide 5 — IGNEOUS: Born of Fire**
Rocks crystallized from molten **magma**.
*   **Intrusive (Plutonic):** Cools slowly underground, forming large crystals (e.g., **Granite**).
*   **Extrusive (Volcanic):** Cools rapidly at the surface, forming small crystals (e.g., **Basalt**).
*(Visual: A split image: one side shows a glossy cut of granite; the other shows the rough texture of a lava flow.)*

**Slide 6 — SEDIMENTARY: The Storybook**
Rocks formed from compressed and cemented **sediment**.
*   **Clastic:** From fragments (e.g., **Sandstone**, **Shale**).
*   **Chemical/Biochemical:** From precipitates or organic remains (e.g., **Limestone**, made from shells).
They are Earth's layered diary.
*(Visual: A photo of clearly defined sedimentary layers in a canyon wall, or a fossiliferous limestone.)*

**Slide 7 — METAMORPHIC: The Transformer**
Existing rocks changed by intense **heat and pressure** without melting.
*   **Limestone → Marble**
*   **Shale → Slate → Schist → Gneiss**
Each new texture and mineral tells a tale of its journey deep within the Earth.
*(Visual: A beautiful side-by-side: rough shale transforming into smooth, foliated slate, then into banded gneiss.)*

**Slide 8 — THE ROCK CYCLE: Earth's Eternal Loop**
No rock is forever. The **Rock Cycle** is the grand process that transforms one rock type into another, powered by Earth's internal heat and the sun's energy. It is the ultimate recycling system.
*(Visual: A clean, central, animated graphic of the classic rock cycle triangle with connecting arrows.)*

**Slide 9 — CYCLE STEP 1: WEATHERING & EROSION**
Wind, water, ice, and life break down all exposed rock into fragments and dissolved ions. Mountains are reduced to sand and clay—the raw materials for new rocks.
*(Visual: A time-lapse style graphic of a mountain being worn down into sediment.)*

**Slide 10 — CYCLE STEP 2: TRANSPORT & DEPOSITION**
Rivers, wind, and glaciers transport the sediment. It is deposited in layers in basins, deltas, and the seafloor, often sorting particles by size.
*(Visual: A satellite image of a massive river delta depositing sediment into the ocean, creating swirling patterns.)*

**Slide 11 — CYCLE STEP 3: BURIAL & LITHIFICATION**
Deep burial compacts the layers. Mineral-rich groundwater cements the grains together, turning loose sediment into solid **sedimentary rock**.
*(Visual: A cross-section diagram showing layers of sand and mud being buried and turning into sandstone and shale.)*

**Slide 12 — CYCLE STEP 4: METAMORPHISM**
Buried deeper or caught in a mountain-building collision, rocks are subjected to immense heat and pressure. Their minerals recrystallize, forming new **metamorphic rocks**.
*(Visual: A diagram of sedimentary rocks being dragged into a subduction zone or mountain root, transforming into schist.)*

**Slide 13 — CYCLE STEP 5: MELTING & NEW MAGMA**
At the deepest and hottest parts of the cycle, rocks can **melt**, becoming magma once more. This magma may rise to form new **igneous rocks**, closing—and restarting—the loop.
*(Visual: A cross-section showing metamorphic rock melting into magma, which then rises toward the surface.)*

**Slide 14 — THE SKIN IS ALIVE**
This constant transformation means Earth's surface is never static. A **volcano** adds new igneous rock. **Erosion** cuts canyons into sedimentary layers. **Mountain building** thrusts up metamorphic cores. The skin breathes, moves, and renews.
*(Visual: A dramatic composite image showcasing all three processes: a volcano, a sedimentary canyon, and a metamorphic mountain peak.)*

This ever-changing skin is heated from below by Earth's internal forge. But on its surface, bathed in sunlight and water, this dynamic canvas gave rise to something even more extraordinary: the interconnected web of life.
