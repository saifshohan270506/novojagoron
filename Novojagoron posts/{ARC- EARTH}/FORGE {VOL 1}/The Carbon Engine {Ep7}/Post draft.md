**Slide 4 — STEP 1: THE FORGE EXHALES (CO₂ SOURCE)**
Volcanoes and mid-ocean ridges release **carbon dioxide (CO₂)** from Earth's mantle through **outgassing**. This is the primary natural source of atmospheric CO₂ over geologic time.
*(Visual: A close-up of a volcanic vent with a highlighted stream of CO₂ molecules rising into the atmosphere.)*

**Slide 5 — STEP 2: ACIDIC RAIN**
Atmospheric CO₂ dissolves in rainwater, forming weak **carbonic acid (H₂CO₃)**. This slightly acidic rain is the primary agent of chemical weathering.
*(Visual: A diagram showing a raindrop forming in a cloud, absorbing a CO₂ molecule, and transforming into a molecule of H₂CO₃ as it falls.)*

**Slide 6 — STEP 3: WEATHERING THE CONTINENTS**
Carbonic acid reacts with **silicate rocks** (e.g., granite), dissolving them. This **chemical weathering** captures atmospheric carbon in dissolved bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻) that are carried to the ocean by rivers.
*(Visual: An animation of acidic rain falling on a granite mountainside, with a simple chemical equation overlay: CaSiO₃ + 2CO₂ + H₂O → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ + SiO₂)*

**Slide 7 — RIVERS: THE CONVEYOR BELTS**
Rivers transport the weathered products—dissolved calcium, magnesium, and bicarbonate ions—to the ocean. The world's great rivers are the arteries of the carbon cycle.
*(Visual: A satellite image of a massive river delta, like the Amazon, spewing sediment and dissolved minerals into the blue ocean.)*

**Slide 8 — STEP 4: LIFE SEQUESTERS (THE CARBONATE PUMP)**
In the sunlit ocean, tiny marine organisms (like **foraminifera** and **coccolithophores**) use the calcium and bicarbonate to build shells and skeletons of **calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)**.
*(Visual: A stunning photomicrograph of a foraminifera shell or a coccolithophore, highlighting its intricate calcium carbonate structure.)*

**Slide 9 — STEP 5: BURIAL IN THE DEEP**
When these organisms die, their carbonate shells sink to the deep ocean floor, forming layers of **limestone** sediment. This **burial** removes carbon from the surface system for millions of years.
*(Visual: A cross-section diagram of the ocean showing a "rain" of white carbonate shells sinking to the seafloor and accumulating as sediment.)*

**Slide 10 — STEP 6: SUBDUCTION & RECYCLING**
Plate tectonics subducts the carbonate-rich oceanic crust back into the mantle. Intense heat and pressure break down the carbonates, releasing CO₂, which rises via magma to feed volcanoes—completing the multi-million-year loop.
*(Visual: A cross-section diagram showing carbonate sediments on a subducting plate, with heat releasing CO₂ that rises into a volcanic arc.)*

**Slide 11 — THE SELF-REGULATING THERMOSTAT**
This creates a stabilizing **negative feedback loop**:
**Warmer Climate → Faster Weathering → More CO₂ Removed → Cooler Climate → Slower Weathering → CO₂ Builds Up → Warmer Climate.**
Earth's climate is kept in balance.
*(Visual: A elegant circular flowchart illustrating this feedback loop with simple icons for temperature, weathering, and CO₂.)*

**Slide 12 — WHEN THE THERMOSTAT IS OVERWHELMED**
Catastrophic events can break the balance. **Large Igneous Province** eruptions (like the Siberian Traps) can release CO₂ too fast, causing extreme global warming and mass extinctions.
*(Visual: A map of the ancient Siberian Traps eruptions with a graph showing a correlating spike in temperature.)*
