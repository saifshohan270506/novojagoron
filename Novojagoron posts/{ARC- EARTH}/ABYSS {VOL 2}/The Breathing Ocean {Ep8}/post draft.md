**Slide 1 — TITLE / HOOK (Dynamic)**
**THE BREATHING OCEAN**
With every wave, the ocean breathes. It inhales carbon dioxide and exhales oxygen, regulates our climate, and absorbs our excess heat. This delicate, planetary respiration has kept Earth in balance for eons. Now, its rhythm is changing.
*(Visual: A serene time-lapse of ocean waves, overlaid with subtle animated O₂ molecules rising and CO₂ molecules sinking into the water.)*

**Slide 2 — THE GREAT EXCHANGE: Sea & Sky**
The ocean's surface is a vast, living membrane where gases are constantly traded with the atmosphere. Cold polar waters act as lungs, deeply **absorbing CO₂**. Warmer, productive waters **release O₂**, much of it produced by the sunlit world's phytoplankton.
*(Visual: A split diagram of the ocean-atmosphere interface, with animated arrows showing the dominant flows of CO₂ into cold seas and O₂ out of productive zones.)*

**Slide 3 — THE SOLUBILITY PUMP: The Cold Sink**
Physics drives the first major carbon sink. **Cold water can hold more dissolved gas.** In the polar regions, chilled surface waters from the **Blue Engine** (circulation) sink, pulling massive amounts of atmospheric CO₂ with them into the deep ocean for centuries.
*(Visual: A globe highlighting the poles, with an animation of CO₂ molecules being drawn into sinking cold water masses.)*

**Slide 4 — THE BIOLOGICAL PUMP: A Living Conveyor**
Life drives the second, active pump. A grand recap:
**Sunlight → Phytoplankton** (absorb CO₂) → **Food Web & DVM** → **Marine Snow** → **Carbon Burial** in the Abyss.
This living conveyor belt moves carbon from the atmosphere to the seafloor vault.
*(Visual: An elegant, integrated graphic linking icons from Episodes 3, 4, 5, and 7 in a continuous loop.)*

**Slide 5 — THE CHEMISTRY OF CHANGE: Ocean Acidification**
When excess CO₂ dissolves, it doesn't just hide; it reacts:
**CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (Carbonic Acid) → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻**
The increase in hydrogen ions (H⁺) makes the seawater more acidic.
*(Visual: A clear, animated molecular equation showing this transformation step-by-step.)*

**Slide 6 — A LOGARITHMIC THREAT: The pH Shift**
pH is a logarithmic scale. Since the Industrial Revolution, the ocean's average pH has dropped from ~8.2 to ~8.1. This 0.1 unit change represents a **30% increase in acidity**. The change is accelerating.
*(Visual: A dramatic pH scale graphic, with arrows showing the shift and emphasizing its logarithmic impact.)*

**Slide 7 — VICTIMS OF ACIDIFICATION: The Shell-Builders**
For organisms that build shells and skeletons from **calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)**, acidic water is corrosive. **Pteropods** (sea butterflies), **corals**, **oysters**, and even some plankton struggle to form and maintain their structures.
*(Visual: A heartbreaking side-by-side microscope image: a healthy, smooth-shelled pteropod next to one from acidic water with a pitted, dissolving shell.)*

**Slide 8 — THE OCEAN'S FEVER: Heat Uptake**
The ocean has been our climate buffer, absorbing **over 90% of the excess heat** trapped by greenhouse gases. This has slowed atmospheric warming but given the ocean a fever, with profound consequences.
*(Visual: A global heat map animation from 1950-present, showing the ocean's surface shifting from blues to oranges and reds.)*

**Slide 9 — CONSEQUENCES OF THE FEVER**
*   **Coral Bleaching:** Stressed corals expel their symbiotic algae, turning white and starving.
*   **Marine Heatwaves:** Devastating kelp forests and fisheries.
*   **Intensified Storms:** Warmer surface waters fuel more powerful hurricanes and typhoons.
*   **Sea Level Rise:** Thermal expansion of warming water.
*(Visual: A four-panel grid showing these impacts: a bleached reef, a graph of a heatwave, a satellite image of a hurricane, and an illustration of thermal expansion.)*

**Slide 10 — WEAKENING THE PUMPS: Dangerous Feedback Loops**
The stresses feed back on themselves:
1.  **Warmer water holds less CO₂**, weakening the solubility pump.
2.  **Acidification and heat stress plankton**, weakening the biological pump.
3.  **Melting ice freshens surface water**, potentially slowing deep circulation (the Blue Engine).
These loops could amplify climate change.
*(Visual: A flowchart diagram illustrating these three interconnected feedback loops.)*

**Slide 11 — THE MEMORY WARNS US (Link to Ep 7)**
The ocean's own sediment archives reveal the truth. Compared to the slow, cyclic CO₂ changes of the past 800,000 years (from ice cores), the modern spike is a vertical line—**unprecedented in both speed and magnitude**.
*(Visual: A stark graph overlaying the slow, wavy paleoclimate CO₂ record with the near-vertical spike of the last 150 years.)*
