**Slide 5 — A CORE REVEALED**
A core is a vertical timeline. Each layer tells a story:
*   **White Layer:** Calcareous ooze—a warm, productive period.
*   **Dark Grey Layer:** Glacial clay—cold, icy conditions.
*   **Sharp Black Line:** Volcanic ash—a precise time marker.
*   **Red Band:** Oxidized iron—a shift in ocean chemistry.
*(Visual: A close-up of a core section with these layers clearly labeled, like a annotated book.)*

**Slide 6 — DECODING ATOMS: The Oxygen Isotope Proxy**
Tiny foraminifera shells lock in a climate signal. During an ice age, lighter oxygen-16 (¹⁶O) is trapped in ice sheets, so seawater becomes richer in heavier oxygen-18 (¹⁸O). Shells record this ratio (δ¹⁸O), revealing past temperature and ice volume.
*(Visual: A simple, elegant diagram showing the fractionation of oxygen isotopes between ice sheets and the ocean, measured in a foram shell.)*

**Slide 7 — THE CLIMATE'S HEARTBEAT**
Plotting δ¹⁸O from a core reveals the **pulse of the ice ages**: a rhythmic, 100,000-year sawtooth pattern of glacial cold and interglacial warmth. This graph is the ocean's memory of Earth's climate cycles.
*(Visual: The iconic "sawtooth" graph of δ¹⁸O over the last 800,000 years, clearly showing the repeated glacial-interglacial cycles.)*

**Slide 8 — THE PACEMAKER: Milankovitch Cycles**
What drives this rhythm? Slow, predictable changes in Earth's orbit:
1.  **Eccentricity:** Shape of Earth's orbit (100,000-year cycle).
2.  **Obliquity:** Tilt of Earth's axis (41,000-year cycle).
3.  **Precession:** Wobble of the axis (23,000-year cycle).
Together, they pace the coming and going of ice ages.
*(Visual: A clean three-part graphic animating each of these three orbital cycles.)*

**Slide 9 — ABRUPT FLIPS: A Warning from the Past**
The record also shows that climate doesn't always change slowly. **Dansgaard-Oeschger events** reveal sudden, dramatic warming spikes—as much as 10°C in decades—followed by slower cooling. The system can **switch** rapidly.
*(Visual: A zoomed-in graph from a Greenland ice core or marine sediment showing one of these sharp, dramatic warming spikes.)*

Link to **FORGE (Vol. 1)**: Thick, dark layers of **organic-rich shale** tell of times when the ocean's **carbon engine** stalled. Widespread ocean anoxia led to massive burial of organic carbon, altering the atmosphere and climate.
