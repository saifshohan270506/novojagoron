**Slide 2 — THE WIND'S FINGERPRINT**

**Slide 3 — THE CORIOLIS TWIST**
Because our planet spins, anything moving freely over its surface—air or water—is deflected. In the Northern Hemisphere, the deflection is to the **right**. In the Southern Hemisphere, to the **left**. This invisible force curves every ocean current and storm.
*(Visual: A simple, elegant animation of a straight-line path curving due to the Coriolis effect, with clear directional arrows for each hemisphere.)*

**Slide 4 — THE SURFACE HIGHWAYS: Gyres**
These giant, slow whirlpools dominate each ocean basin. The **North Pacific Gyre**, for instance, rotates clockwise, its boundaries defined by fast currents like the Kuroshio. At its calm, stagnant center, debris accumulates—the **Great Pacific Garbage Patch**.

**Slide 5 — THE GREAT CONVEYOR BELT: Thermohaline Circulation**
Beyond the wind-driven surface lies a deeper, slower, and more powerful engine: the **Thermohaline Circulation**. This global conveyor belt, driven by differences in water density, moves water through the deep ocean over **millennia**.
*(Visual: The hero graphic—a stylized, animated cross-section of the world ocean showing cold, dense water sinking in the polar regions, flowing through the abyss, and slowly rising to complete the loop.)*

**Slide 6 — WHAT MAKES WATER SINK?**
Seawater density is governed by **temperature** and **salinity**.
*   **Warm, fresh water** is light and floats.
*   **Cold, salty water** is dense and sinks.
The densest waters form in the icy polar oceans, where sea ice formation leaves behind saltier, heavier water that plunges into the abyss.
*(Visual: A clear diagram comparing two water columns with different temperatures and salinities, showing one sinking and one floating.)*

**Slide 7 — THE ATLANTIC'S HEAT PUMP: Gulf Stream**
The **Gulf Stream** is a mighty river within the sea, part of the Atlantic's conveyor. It transports immense heat from the tropics to the North Atlantic. This is why **London** is milder than **Newfoundland**, which lies at the same latitude.
*(Visual: A map showing the Gulf Stream's warm path, with an inset comparing the average temperatures of London and St. John's, Newfoundland.)*

**Slide 8 — FERTILE UPWELLING: Gardens of the Ocean**
Where winds push surface water away from a coast, deep, cold, nutrient-rich water rises to replace it—a process called **upwelling**. These zones, like those off Peru or California, are the ocean's most fertile grounds, supporting immense plankton blooms and legendary fisheries.
*(Visual: A diagram of coastal upwelling, paired with a satellite image of a vibrant green phytoplankton bloom along a coastline.)*
